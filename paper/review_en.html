AI Research from 2000 to 2026: A Metadata-Driven Citation Map

Generated: 2026-06-27 · Dataset: 2,700 selected papers

Abstract

This draft review maps AI research from 2000 through 2026, investigating up to 1,000 candidate papers per year from free public Semantic Scholar metadata and selecting the top 100 papers from each year by citation count (2,700 papers total). The resulting collection is organized by research taxonomy and enriched with deterministic key ideas, strengths, limitations, and AI-specific keyword tags.

1. Scope and Methods

For each year, broad AI-oriented queries were sent to Semantic Scholar Academic Graph bulk search. Records were retained when title, abstract, or venue metadata matched explicit AI relevance expressions, deduplicated by DOI, arXiv, PubMed, CorpusId, paperId, then title, and reduced to at most 1,000 candidates per year by citation count. The final collection selects the top 100 papers within each publication year by citation count, with influential citation count and metadata importance score retained as tie-breakers and audit signals.

2. Key Findings

  • The 2,700 selected papers account for 8,567,142 citations in the selected set, with the largest citation mass in 2015.
  • The largest category is General AI Methods and Systems (688 papers), reflecting the influence of large-scale model and representation work.
  • The period connects vision transformers, LLMs, retrieval augmentation, self-supervised learning, diffusion/generative AI, trustworthy AI, and AI-for-science workflows.
  • Papers from 2026 are structurally citation-disadvantaged because the year is partial and citation accumulation is still immature.

7. Repository Analyses: AI Research Writing Tooling

Two recent GitHub projects extend this citation map from published AI papers to the surrounding research-production workflow. Leey21/awesome-ai-research-writing is a curated prompt and agent-skill resource for academic writing, while google-research/paper-orchestra is an executable multi-agent framework for transforming raw research materials into LaTeX manuscripts.

RepositoryPrimary roleObserved strengthsLimitations and risks
Leey21/awesome-ai-research-writingPrompt and skill curation for AI-assisted research writingProvides practical templates for LaTeX and Word translation, bilingual polishing, abbreviation, expansion, reviewer-style critique, figure/table captions, experiment analysis, model selection, and agent-skill onboarding. Its repository structure is intentionally lightweight, centered on README guidance and supporting images, which makes the material easy to inspect and reuse.The project is closer to a living writing playbook than a reproducible software artifact. As observed from repository metadata, no explicit license is attached, and the repository does not include an evaluation harness, versioned benchmark, or executable validation scripts for measuring whether each prompt improves paper quality.
google-research/paper-orchestraMulti-agent automation for full paper draftingImplements a pipeline with outline, literature-review, section-writing, content-refinement, and optional plotting agents. The codebase includes a CLI, Streamlit frontend, CVPR/ICLR LaTeX templates, authoring utilities, PDF helpers, and autorater scripts, making it closer to an end-to-end system than a prompt collection.The system depends on external LLM and scholarly-search services, including OpenAI, Gemini/Vertex AI, and Semantic Scholar keys. The README also notes that its dataset is not included, so full reproduction requires additional materials, API configuration, and careful cost, privacy, and citation-quality controls.

Conceptually, the repositories occupy complementary positions. The Leey21 project captures tacit writing practice in copyable prompt formats and is valuable for human-in-the-loop drafting, especially when researchers need local control over wording, translation, reviewer simulation, or figure-caption style. PaperOrchestra instead formalizes a pipeline view of paper production, where raw ideas and experiment logs are routed through specialized agents and compiled into a target conference template.

For the broader AI research landscape, these projects signal a shift from model-centric progress alone toward workflow infrastructure: literature search, citation grounding, drafting, revision, plotting, and submission formatting are becoming first-class targets for automation. Their main caution is that writing fluency should not be mistaken for scientific validity. Any automated writing workflow still needs independent checks for experimental correctness, citation fidelity, novelty, authorship policy, and disclosure requirements.

8. Limitations

This is a metadata-driven citation map rather than a full systematic review of every PDF. Citation count is a useful influence signal, but it is not a direct measure of methodological quality, safety, or reproducibility.

9. Conclusion

AI research is expanding around large-scale models and data-centric methods while simultaneously increasing pressure for safety, interpretability, domain validation, and reproducible evaluation.
